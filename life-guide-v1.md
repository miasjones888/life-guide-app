# Life Guide — v1
**A User Guide Built for Your Brain**
Paper-first. Minimum viable. Designed to reduce friction, not add to it.

This document is your complete reference. Read it once. Then keep it somewhere you can open it when the system feels wobbly. You do not have to memorize it. You do not have to implement everything at once. You just have to do the next small thing.

---

## What This System Is

This is not a productivity system. It is not a framework for doing more. It is a set of minimal structures designed to do one thing: make the next right move obvious, at any given moment, with as little cognitive cost as possible.

It works with how your brain actually operates — not against it. That means it accounts for variable energy, initiation difficulty, overwhelm, interest that drops fast, and the very real cost of too many open loops. It does not require daily maintenance. It does not punish you for missing a day or a week. It does not assume you will feel motivated. It just stays there, ready, whenever you come back to it.

### The Core Principle

Reduce the number of decisions required to begin anything to near zero. The question "what should I work on" is the thing that breaks sessions before they start. This system answers that question in advance, at the lowest possible stakes moment, so that starting becomes: open the folder, do the thing.

### What This System Is Not

- **Not comprehensive.** It does not try to capture everything. It captures enough.
- **Not digital.** Paper is primary. Google Calendar handles only what requires time and a reminder.
- **Not rigid.** The structures are loose by design. They bend without breaking.
- **Not punishing.** When it breaks — and it will — you come back without ceremony. No catch-up, no guilt, no restart ritual. Just open the notebook and write tomorrow's task.

### Tasks Are Modular

Everything in this system is moveable. If a deep focus session needs to shift because of a Google meeting, it shifts. If the weekly setup happens Monday instead of Sunday, that is fine. If laundry moves from Saturday to Tuesday, the week still works. The blocks are anchors, not walls. When something unexpected appears on the calendar, you rearrange around it — you do not abandon the system. One reschedule is not a failure. The system flexes.

The only things that do not move: cat medication alarms, your own medication reminders, and financial deadlines. Everything else is negotiable.

## What You Own

This is the complete physical and digital inventory of your system. Nothing else. If something is not on this list, it is not part of the system.

### Paper

**The Main Notebook — Large**

This is your primary brain. Everything goes in it. Project thoughts, stray ideas, things you need to do, things you are thinking about, responses to what you are reading. No sections. No tabs. No index. No rules about what goes where.

The only recurring use: the night before, write one sentence — the single thing that makes tomorrow a good enough day if it gets done. That is the only discipline the main notebook requires.

When it fills up, start a new one. Do not transfer content unless something is actively unfinished and you need it in front of you. The old notebook goes on a shelf. You are not going back to it.

**The Small Notebook — Morning Pages**

Lives on your bedside or desk. Used exclusively for your 20-minute morning writing practice. Nothing else goes in it. When it is full, shelf it unread. You are not mining it. You are clearing the pipes.

**The Monthly Paper Calendar**

One page per month. Appointments and intentions only. No task lists. This gives you the aerial view — what is coming, what the month looks like, how much white space exists. When you look at it and feel overwhelmed, that is information. When you look at it and feel calm, that is also information.

**Index Cards**

Loose. Kept in a small box or a rubber-banded stack. One idea per card. These are for capturing things that want more than a notebook line but less than a full document: a stray philosophical thought, a research thread, an image, a question to investigate later, a fragment for a story. When a card is actionable, it becomes part of a project. When it is not yet actionable, it waits in the stack. You review the stack during Monthly Reset.

**Project Folders**

One physical folder per active project. Currently three:
- **Portfolio** — the container for all portfolio build work, drafts, reference pieces, and output decisions
- **Field Guide** — the container for the Field Guide project build
- **Curriculum Tracker** — the container for the curriculum tracker tool build

Each folder contains: printed research, handwritten notes that belong to that project, index cards pulled for that project, any reference materials. When you are working on a project, the folder is open on your desk. When you are not, it is closed. The physical act of opening and closing is doing organizational work without requiring you to maintain a taxonomy.

**Resource Folder**

One additional folder for reference materials that are not tied to a specific project: the Field Guide, frameworks you return to, reference maps, general research. This is the reading pile. It does not need to be organized. You know what is in it.

**Running Lists (in back of Main Notebook)**

- **Must Buy List** — things that need to be purchased when money allows. Not urgent. Not a shopping cart. Just a record so nothing stays in working memory.
- **Grocery List** — building throughout the week, used for Instacart order on Sunday.

### Digital

**Google Calendar** — for alarms and reminders only. Not for task management. Not for planning. Just: at this time, this thing fires.

**Notes App** — for capturing on the go. Whatever comes up when you are out — ideas, phrases, observations, things you need to remember. These transfer to index cards during Sunday evening setup.

## Project Folders in Depth

### Active Project Slots

The system allows a maximum of 3 active project slots at once. Currently:

1. **Portfolio** (Type 1 — Build) — unlocks job applications when complete
2. **Field Guide** (Type 1 — Build) — becomes project navigator when done
3. **Curriculum Tracker** (Type 1 — Build) — when done, opens a Type 2 slot for the Chthonic Archive curriculum

### Three Types of Project

**Type 1 — Build Project**
You are constructing something that does not exist yet. A tool, a portfolio, a tracker, a thesis with a defined deliverable. Has a folder, a weekly move, and a project slot. Has an end state. When it is done, the slot frees.

Examples: Portfolio, Field Guide, Curriculum Tracker as a tool, the cat archive thesis, a finished chapbook.

**Type 2 — Structured Learning Project**
You are moving through a curriculum, course, or program with readings, exercises, and deliverables. Same system footprint as a build: folder, weekly move, slot, end state. When the curriculum is complete, the slot frees.

The Chthonic Archive curriculum when you begin working through it is Type 2. It cannot open until the Curriculum Tracker build (Type 1) frees a slot.

**Type 3 — Practice**
No folder. No slot. No weekly move. No deliverable. No end state. Practices are recurring conditions of the week — morning pages, sitting, studio time, birdwatching. They generate creative work and index cards that flow into active project folders but do not consume a project slot.

The Applied Buddhism class currently sits closer to Type 2 — it has structure and a defined year. When the course ends and you continue on your own terms, it becomes Type 3.

| Type | Folder | Slot | Weekly Move | End State |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Build | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Deliverable |
| 2 — Structured Learning | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Curriculum complete |
| 3 — Practice | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | None |

**Combined ceiling: max 3 active slots total (Type 1 + Type 2 combined). Soft limit: 2 structured commitments at once.**

### Limiting Practices So They Do Not Stack

The soft limit is two structured learning commitments running simultaneously. Morning pages and sitting do not count. Reading and ambient studio time do not count. Formal classes and curriculums do.

Currently active:
- Applied Buddhism class — Saturdays (Type 2, transitioning to Type 3 when year ends)
- Sculpture class — Tuesdays, May 19 to June 23rd (Type 2)

Activating when Curriculum Tracker build closes:
- Chthonic Archive curriculum (Type 2)

A practice that generates guilt, that you feel behind on, or that you are managing rather than moving through has crossed into obligation. It is now a project without a folder. When that happens: pause it, give it a folder and a slot if it needs structure, or let it go.

### Not All Projects Become Practices

The Chthonic Archive curriculum becomes a practice when you move through it. The cat archive project does not. The cat archive is thesis research building toward a deliverable — a finished piece of work, a scholarly argument, a published or exhibitable thing. When that deliverable exists, the project closes. No practice follows it. A slot opens.

## Maintaining the System

**The 4-Week Rule**

If you want to add, change, or remove something from the routine — a habit, a time block, a practice — you must have tried the current version for at least four weeks before the change is valid. This applies to: morning routine timing, the anchor task system, the deep focus session days, the weekly setup format, any recurring practice.

**What a Month Actually Looks Like**

A month includes: days you feel good and the system runs smoothly. Days you feel terrible and you do one thing or nothing. Days you forget. Days you dismiss the alarms. Days you write the anchor task and then do not do it. Days you do the anchor task and nothing else. All of those are inside a month. The system does not require perfection. It requires returning.

**When the System Feels Broken**

Before changing anything, ask:
- Did the system break, or did a hard month break my relationship with the system?
- What actually didn't work — versus what just felt hard?
- What surprised me about what did work?
- What did I consistently skip, and why?
- Is the thing I want to change genuinely not working, or do I just want the relief of changing something?

## Give It a Month Before You Change Anything

This system was built carefully, with your brain specifically in mind. Before you adjust it, change it, simplify it, or abandon it — give it a full month. Not a perfect month. A real one, with bad days and gaps and stretches where nothing got done.

**The Questions to Ask at One Month (May 1st):**
- Did the system break, or did a hard month break my relationship with the system?
- What actually didn't work — versus what just felt hard?
- What surprised me about what did work?
- What did I consistently skip, and why?
- Is the thing I want to change genuinely not working, or do I just want the relief of changing something?

**The Four-Week Rule for Routines:**
If you want to add, change, or remove something from the routine, you must have tried the current version for at least four weeks before the change is valid.

## How to Update This System

The system is a living document. Changes happen through a deliberate process.

### The Update Protocol

1. Wait until you have run the current version for at least 4 weeks
2. Open a new Claude conversation
3. Share two things:
   - The current user guide document
   - A prompt describing what you want to change and why
4. Workshop the change together
5. Update the calendar events and the guide document
6. Run the new version for 4 weeks before evaluating it

### What Counts as a Change

A change is: adding a new recurring habit, removing an existing one, shifting the timing of a practice, adding a new active project folder, updating the prioritization list, or significantly altering the weekly or monthly structure.

A change is NOT: skipping something on a bad day, adjusting one week because of an unusual schedule, or choosing a different coffee shop.

### One Change at a Time

When you do update the system, change one thing at a time. Change the most important one. Run it for four weeks. Evaluate. Then change the next thing if needed.

## Research and Project States

Research is not a phase you complete before building. It is a practice that runs alongside everything else — sometimes embedded in a project, sometimes freestanding, sometimes just following curiosity with no destination yet.

### Three Research States

**1. Freestanding Research (No Active Project)**
Research that is not attached to any current project. Lives in the Resource Folder and on index cards. Follows curiosity. No deadline, no deliverable. Transferred to cards during Sunday setup.

**2. Build-Embedded Research**
The Chthonic Archive worldbuilding. The curriculum sources. Theory reading that is the work itself, not preparation for it. This research does not have an end state — it grows as the project grows.

**3. Project-Adjacent Research**
Research gathered in service of a specific deliverable — the portfolio, the Field Guide. This is more bounded: you are looking for specific things, and when you find them, you stop.

### The Weekly Research Block

The research time block in your weekly schedule is flexible. Some weeks it feeds an active project. Some weeks it is pure exploration with no destination. Both are valid. The only thing it is not for: opening a new active project when the three slots are full.

## Creative Projects: Where Research Lives and How to Start

### The Gap Between Idea and Page

The central problem with creative writing is not lack of ideas. It is the distance between having an idea and doing the work of writing it. This system addresses that distance by making the writing move small enough to not require motivation.

**The smallest completable version of a creative project:**
1. Name it (one sentence — what is this?)
2. Identify the constraint (how long? how many sections?)
3. Write the first bad version
4. Identify the gap (what is missing?)
5. Write toward the gaps, not the pieces you already have

**The Index Card Start:**
Before writing anything in long form, put the project on an index card. Write:
- What is this? (One sentence)
- What is the smallest version of it that would be complete?
- What is the first sentence?

When the card has those three things, the project is ready to begin. Not before.

## How to Set Up Notecards and Journal Pages

### The Notecard System

**Index cards (3x5 or 4x6):**
- One idea per card, always
- Write on the front only
- Date it lightly in the corner if helpful
- Do not organize them — the stack IS the organization
- Pull a card when it becomes relevant to a project
- Put it in the project folder when it is actively in use
- Return it to the stack when the project closes (or archive it)

**Types of card:**
- Fragment cards (quotes, phrases, images in words)
- Question cards (questions to investigate)
- Reference cards (book, film, article you want to find)
- Map cards (structural diagrams of a project)
- Research cards (a finding with a source note)

### Notecard Layouts

**Fragment / Phrase Card:**
```
[Date corner]
"The phrase or fragment exactly as it came"
— context or source if known
```

**Question Card:**
```
[Date corner]
Q: What is the relationship between [X] and [Y]?
Why it matters: one sentence
```

**Project Map Card:**
```
[Project name]
[What it is — one sentence]
[Sections or shape]
[What is missing]
```

### Journal Page Layouts

**Morning Pages page (small notebook):**
No layout. Write continuously. Do not edit. Do not re-read. 20 minutes.

**Anchor Task Page (main notebook, nightly):**
```
Tomorrow's one thing:
[Single sentence — the thing that makes tomorrow a good enough day]
```

## Habit Stacking and Starting Without Forcing It

**The principle:** Motivation follows action more reliably than it precedes it. You do not wait until you feel like working. You do the smallest possible thing and let the feeling catch up.

**Morning pages practice is built on exactly this:** you write before you feel like writing, and the writing makes you feel like writing.

**Habit stacks that work:**

| Trigger | Stack |
|---|---|
| Morning alarm | Sit up → small notebook → write one sentence → that is the session if necessary |
| Coffee is brewing | Open the project folder |
| Sit down at coffee shop | Phone in bag → document open → first sentence |
| Cat meds alarm fires | Do meds → check the anchor task for the day |
| Sunday 7pm alarm | Open calendar → write three intentions → done |

**The 2-minute rule for starting:**
If the task takes less than 2 minutes to start — sit down, open the folder, write the first word — do it now. The resistance is not about the task. It is about the threshold. Lower the threshold.

## Using Index Cards for Creative Projects

Index cards are the unit of creative thinking in this system. Not notebooks. Not documents. Cards.

**Why cards:**
- They are small. Smallness is the point.
- They do not demand a complete thought. Fragments live here.
- They are physical. Moving them is a form of editing.
- They accumulate without requiring organization.

**The session card:**

For sculpture, Buddhism class, or any practice session where you want to capture something:

```
[Date] [Session type]
What I noticed:
What I want to remember:
One question it opened:
```

That is the entire format. Fill in whatever applies. Leave the rest blank.

**Pulling cards for a project:**

When you begin a project, pull all cards that feel relevant. Spread them on a surface. Notice which ones cluster. The clusters are structure. The structure is the outline. You did not invent it — you excavated it from what you had already thought.
